## Historical Events

### Pre-European & Aboriginal

- **~15,000 years ago** — Ancestors of Aboriginal peoples migrate from Asia and settle across what is now Canada. They live off the land — some by hunting and gathering, others by raising crops.
  - Groups: Huron-Wendat (farmers/hunters, Great Lakes), Cree & Dene (hunter-gatherers, Northwest), Sioux (nomadic, followed bison), Inuit (Arctic wildlife), West Coast peoples (preserved fish by drying/smoking)
  - Warfare was common among Aboriginal groups competing for land, resources and prestige.

- **~1000 AD** — Vikings from Iceland, who had colonized Greenland, reach Labrador and the island of Newfoundland. Their settlement, **L'Anse aux Meadows**, is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

---

### Age of Exploration

- **1497** — **John Cabot** (Italian immigrant to England) becomes the first European to map Canada's East Coast, setting foot on Newfoundland or Cape Breton Island. He claims the land for England as the "New Founde Land." English settlement does not begin until 1610.

- **1534–1542** — **Jacques Cartier** makes three voyages across the Atlantic, claiming the land for King Francis I of France. He explores the St. Lawrence River and sets eyes on present-day Québec City and Montreal. He hears two captured guides speak the Iroquoian word *kanata* (meaning "village") — by the 1550s, the name "Canada" begins appearing on maps.

---

### New France

- **1604** — The first European settlement north of Florida is established by French explorers **Pierre de Monts** and **Samuel de Champlain** — first on St. Croix Island (present-day Maine), then at **Port-Royal** in Acadia (present-day Nova Scotia). The descendants of these French colonists are the **Acadians**.

- **1608** — **Samuel de Champlain** builds a fortress at what is now **Québec City**. He allies the colony with the Algonquin, Montagnais and Huron against the Iroquois Confederacy.

- **1670** — **King Charles II** of England grants the **Hudson's Bay Company** exclusive trading rights over the watershed draining into Hudson Bay. For the next 100 years the Company competes with Montreal-based traders. French and Aboriginal peoples collaborate in the vast fur-trade economy driven by demand for beaver pelts in Europe.

- **1701** — The French and the Iroquois make peace, ending a century of conflict.

- **1755–1763** — During the war between Britain and France, more than two-thirds of the **Acadians** are deported from their homeland — an ordeal known as the **"Great Upheaval."** Despite this, Acadian culture survives and flourishes. New Brunswick remains the only officially bilingual province today.

- **1759** — The British defeat the French at the **Battle of the Plains of Abraham** at Québec City, marking the end of France's empire in North America. Both commanders — British **Brigadier James Wolfe** and French **Marquis de Montcalm** — are killed in battle.

---

### British Rule & Loyalists

- **1763** — Following the war, Great Britain renames the colony the **"Province of Quebec."** The **Royal Proclamation of 1763** by **King George III** first guarantees Aboriginal territorial rights.

- **1774** — The British Parliament passes the **Quebec Act of 1774** — one of the constitutional foundations of Canada. It allows religious freedom for Catholics, permits them to hold public office, and restores French civil law while maintaining British criminal law.

- **1775** — **Sir Guy Carleton (Lord Dorchester)**, Governor of Quebec, defeats an American military invasion of Quebec.

- **1776** — The 13 British colonies to the south declare independence, forming the **United States of America**. North America is again divided by war.

- **1776–1783** — More than **40,000 Loyalists** — people loyal to the Crown — flee the American Revolution to settle in Nova Scotia and Quebec. **Joseph Brant** leads thousands of Loyalist Mohawk Indians into Canada. About **3,000 Black Loyalists** come north seeking a better life.

- **1782–1783** — Sir Guy Carleton supervises the Loyalist migration to Nova Scotia and Quebec.

- **1791** — The **Constitutional Act of 1791** divides the Province of Quebec into:
  - **Upper Canada** (later Ontario) — mainly Loyalist, Protestant, English-speaking
  - **Lower Canada** (later Quebec) — heavily Catholic and French-speaking
  - Both are granted legislative assemblies elected by the people — the first in Canada. The name "Canada" becomes official.

- **1792** — Some Black Nova Scotians, given poor land after the Revolution, move to establish **Freetown, Sierra Leone** (West Africa) — a new British colony for freed slaves.

- **1793** — **Upper Canada**, led by Lieutenant Governor **John Graves Simcoe**, becomes the first province in the British Empire to move toward **abolition of slavery**. Also the year the first elected Assembly of Lower Canada meets in Québec City.

- **1807** — The British Parliament prohibits the buying and selling of slaves.

- **1833** — The British Parliament **abolishes slavery** throughout the Empire. Thousands of slaves escape from the United States, follow "the North Star," and settle in Canada via the **Underground Railroad**, a Christian anti-slavery network.

---

### War of 1812

- **June 1812** — Believing it would be easy to conquer Canada, the **United States launches an invasion**. Canadian volunteers, First Nations and British soldiers unite to defend Canada.

- **July 1812** — **Major-General Sir Isaac Brock** captures Detroit but is killed while defending against an American attack at **Queenston Heights**, near Niagara Falls. The Americans lose the battle.

- **1813** — **Lieutenant-Colonel Charles de Salaberry** and 460 soldiers (mostly French Canadians) turn back 4,000 American invaders at **Châteauguay**, south of Montreal.

- **1813** — The Americans burn **Government House and the Parliament Buildings in York** (now Toronto). **HMS Shannon** captures the USS Chesapeake and leads it into Halifax harbour.

- **1813** — **Laura Secord**, pioneer wife and mother of five, makes a dangerous **19-mile (30 km) journey on foot** to warn Lieutenant James FitzGibbon of a planned American attack. Her bravery contributes to victory at the **Battle of Beaver Dams**. She is recognized as a Canadian heroine.

- **1814** — In retaliation for York, **Major-General Robert Ross** leads an expedition from Nova Scotia that **burns the White House** and other public buildings in Washington, D.C. Ross dies in battle soon after and is buried in Halifax with full military honours.

- **1814** — The American attempt to conquer Canada fails. The **Duke of Wellington** sends his best soldiers to defend Canada and chooses **Bytown (Ottawa)** as the endpoint of the Rideau Canal — playing a direct role in founding the national capital. The present-day **Canada–U.S.A. border** is partly an outcome of the War of 1812, which ensured Canada would remain independent.

---

### Road to Confederation

- **1832** — The **Montreal Stock Exchange** opens.

- **1837–1838** — **Rebellions in Upper and Lower Canada** occur outside Montreal and in Toronto. Reformers believe progress toward full democracy is too slow. The rebels are defeated by British troops and Canadian volunteers. **Lord Durham** is sent to report on the rebellions.

- **1840** — Upper and Lower Canada are united as the **Province of Canada**. Reformers **Sir Louis-Hippolyte La Fontaine** and **Robert Baldwin** work toward responsible government.

- **1847–1848** — **Nova Scotia** becomes the first British North American colony to attain **full responsible government**.

- **1848–1849** — **Lord Elgin**, governor of United Canada, introduces responsible government. **Sir Louis-Hippolyte La Fontaine** becomes the first leader of a responsible government in the Canadas — a champion of French language rights.

- **1853** — **Mary Ann Shadd Cary** becomes the first woman publisher in Canada, helping found *The Provincial Freeman* — a weekly newspaper dedicated to anti-slavery, Black immigration to Canada and temperance.

- **1858** — First elected Assembly in **Halifax, Nova Scotia** (1758 is the original date given in the guide).

---

### Confederation & Expansion

- **1864–1867** — Representatives of Nova Scotia, New Brunswick and the Province of Canada work together as the **Fathers of Confederation** to establish a new country with two levels of government: federal and provincial.

- **July 1, 1867** — The British Parliament passes the **British North America Act**. The **Dominion of Canada** is officially born with **Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia and New Brunswick** as founding provinces. **Sir John A. Macdonald** becomes Canada's first Prime Minister. Until 1982 this day was "Dominion Day"; today it is **Canada Day**.

- **1869** — Canada takes over the vast northwest from the Hudson's Bay Company. The 12,000 **Métis of the Red River** are not consulted. **Louis Riel** leads an armed uprising and seizes Fort Garry.

- **1870** — Ottawa sends soldiers to retake Fort Garry. Riel flees to the United States. **Manitoba** becomes a province.

- **1871** — **British Columbia** joins Canada after Ottawa promises to build a railway to the West Coast.

- **1873** — **Prince Edward Island** joins Canada. Prime Minister Macdonald establishes the **North West Mounted Police (NWMP)** to pacify the West. The NWMP founds Fort Calgary, Fort MacLeod and other centres; **Regina** becomes headquarters.

- **1880** — Transfer of the **Arctic Islands** to the Northwest Territories.

- **November 7, 1885** — **Donald Smith (Lord Strathcona)** drives the **last spike** of the **Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR)**, completing the railway from sea to sea. The project is built by both European and Chinese labour. The Chinese are afterward subjected to the discriminatory **Head Tax**.

- **1885** — **Second Métis Rebellion** in present-day Saskatchewan. **Louis Riel** is tried and executed for high treason — a decision strongly opposed in Quebec. He is seen by many as a hero and the **father of Manitoba**. **Gabriel Dumont** is the Métis' greatest military leader in this conflict.

- **1891** — **James Naismith**, a Canadian, invents **basketball**.

- **1898** — **Yukon Territory** established.

- **1899–1902** — **South African War (Boer War)**: over 7,000 Canadians volunteer; over 260 die. Canadians fight at the battles of **Paardeberg** and **Lillefontein**, strengthening national pride.

- **1905** — **Alberta** and **Saskatchewan** become provinces.

- **1921** — **King George V** assigns Canada's national colours — **red and white** — the colours of the national flag today.

---

### Moving Westward & Women's Rights

- **Late 1800s–1914** — Sir **Wilfrid Laurier**, first French-Canadian Prime Minister since Confederation, encourages immigration to the West. The railway enables 170,000 Ukrainians, 115,000 Poles and tens of thousands from Germany, France, Norway and Sweden to settle in the West.

- **1916** — **Manitoba** becomes the first province to grant **voting rights to women**.

- **1917** — The federal government of **Sir Robert Borden** gives women the right to vote in federal elections — first to nurses at the front, then to women related to men in active wartime service.

- **1918** — Most Canadian female citizens aged 21 and over are granted the right to vote in federal elections. **Dr. Emily Stowe** (first Canadian woman to practise medicine) is the founder of the **women's suffrage movement** in Canada.

- **1921** — **Agnes Macphail**, a farmer and teacher, becomes the **first woman MP**.

- **1940** — Thanks to the work of **Thérèse Casgrain** and others, **Quebec** grants women the provincial vote.

---

### First World War

- **1914** — Germany attacks Belgium and France; Britain declares war. Ottawa forms the **Canadian Expeditionary Force**. More than **600,000 Canadians serve** out of a total population of eight million — most of them volunteers.

- **1914–1920** — Ottawa interns over **8,000 former Austro-Hungarian subjects** (mainly Ukrainian men) as "enemy aliens" in 24 labour camps across Canada.

- **1915** — Lieutenant-Colonel **John McCrae** composes the poem **"In Flanders Fields"** — recited every Remembrance Day.

- **April 9, 1917** — The **Canadian Corps captures Vimy Ridge**, with 10,000 killed or wounded, securing Canadians' reputation as "the shock troops of the British Empire." One Canadian officer said: *"It was Canada from the Atlantic to the Pacific on parade… In those few minutes I witnessed the birth of a nation."* **April 9 is celebrated as Vimy Day.**

- **August 8, 1918** — The **Battle of Amiens** — described by Germany as "the black day of the German Army." Under **General Sir Arthur Currie** (Canada's greatest soldier), the Canadian Corps advances in the final **Hundred Days Offensive**, through Arras, Canal du Nord, Cambrai and Mons.

- **November 11, 1918** — **Armistice** — the First World War ends. In total, **60,000 Canadians were killed and 170,000 wounded**. Every year on **November 11 at the 11th hour**, Canadians observe **Remembrance Day**, wearing the red poppy to honour over one million who served and the 110,000 who gave their lives.

---

### Between the Wars

- **1920** — The **Group of Seven** is founded — Canadian artists who develop a distinctive style painting rugged wilderness landscapes.

- **1921** — The British Empire evolves into a free association known as the **British Commonwealth of Nations**.

- **1927** — **Old Age Security** is devised.

- **1929** — The **stock market crash** leads to the **Great Depression** ("the Dirty Thirties"). Unemployment reaches 27% by 1933. Western Canadian farmers are hit hardest.

- **1933** — Unemployment peaks at **27%**. Growing demand for a social safety net.

- **1934** — The **Bank of Canada** is created to manage the money supply.

- **1939** — Immigration drops; many refugees including **Jews fleeing Nazi Germany** are turned away.

---

### Second World War

- **1939** — **Adolf Hitler** (Nazi dictator of Germany) invades Poland and conquers much of Europe. Canada joins the democratic allies. More than **one million Canadians and Newfoundlanders serve** out of a population of 11.5 million; **44,000 are killed**.

- **1940** — **Unemployment Insurance** introduced by the federal government.

- **1941** — Canadians suffer losses in the unsuccessful defence of **Hong Kong** from Imperial Japan.

- **1942** — Failed raid on **Dieppe**, Nazi-controlled coast of France.

- **1940–1945** — The **Royal Canadian Air Force (RCAF)** takes part in the Battle of Britain. Canada contributes more to the Allied air effort than any other Commonwealth country, training over **130,000 Allied air crew** under the British Commonwealth Air Training Plan.

- **1940–1945** — The **Royal Canadian Navy (RCN)** sees its finest hour in the **Battle of the Atlantic**, protecting merchant ship convoys against German submarines. By war's end, Canada has the **third-largest navy in the world**.

- **1943–1944** — Canadians take part in the **liberation of Italy**.

- **June 6, 1944 — D-Day** — In the epic **invasion of Normandy**, **15,000 Canadian troops storm and capture Juno Beach**. Approximately one in ten Allied soldiers on D-Day is Canadian.

- **1944–1945** — The **Canadian Army liberates the Netherlands**. Germans surrender **May 8, 1945** (V-E Day), ending six years of war in Europe.

- **August 14, 1945** — **Japan surrenders**, ending the war in the Pacific.

- **Wartime injustice** — Canadians of **Japanese origin** are forcibly relocated and their property sold without compensation, despite the military and RCMP advising Ottawa they posed little danger. The **Government of Canada apologized in 1988** and compensated victims.

---

### Modern Canada

- **1947** — **Oil is discovered in Alberta**, beginning Canada's modern energy industry.

- **1948** — **Japanese-Canadians** gain the right to vote — the last group of Canadians of Asian descent denied the vote.

- **1949** — **Newfoundland and Labrador** joins Canada as the 10th province.

- **1950–1953** — **Korean War**: Canada participates in the UN operation defending South Korea. 500 dead, 1,000 wounded.

- **1951** — For the first time, a majority of Canadians can afford adequate food, shelter and clothing.

- **1956** — Canada welcomes approximately **37,000 refugees** who escaped Soviet tyranny in Hungary.

- **1960** — **Aboriginal people are granted the right to vote**.

- **1963** — Parliament establishes the **Royal Commission on Bilingualism and Biculturalism**.

- **1965** — **Canada and Quebec Pension Plans** established.

- **1969** — The **Official Languages Act** guarantees French and English services in the federal government across Canada.

- **1970** — Canada helps found **La Francophonie**, an international association of French-speaking countries.

- **1972** — **Paul Henderson** scores the winning goal for Canada in the **Canada–Soviet Summit Series** — often called *"the goal heard around the world."*

- **1975** — With the Communist victory in the Vietnam War, over **50,000 Vietnamese refugees** find refuge in Canada.

- **1980** — The **Quebec sovereignty referendum** is defeated. **Terry Fox**, who lost his right leg to cancer at 18, begins the **"Marathon of Hope"** — a cross-country run to raise money for cancer research. He becomes a national hero.

- **1982** — The **Constitution is amended**. The **Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms** is entrenched. Quebec does not agree to the amendment.

- **1985** — **Rick Hansen** circles the globe in a wheelchair to raise funds for spinal cord research.

- **1988** — The Government of Canada apologizes for Japanese-Canadian wartime internment and compensates victims.

- **1989** — The **Canadian Space Agency** is founded. Canadian astronauts begin participating in space exploration using the **Canadarm** (robotic arm designed and built in Canada).

- **1995** — The **second Quebec sovereignty referendum** is defeated.

- **1996** — **Donovan Bailey** wins a world record sprint and **double Olympic gold** at the Atlanta Summer Games.

- **1999** — **Nunavut** ("our land" in Inuktitut) is established from the eastern part of the Northwest Territories.

- **2002** — **Catriona Le May Doan** carries the flag after winning Olympic gold in speed skating at the Salt Lake City Winter Games.

- **2006** — The Government of Canada **apologizes for the Chinese Head Tax** — a race-based entry fee imposed after Chinese workers helped build the CPR.

## Canadian Symbols & Key Facts

### Government
- Canada is a **constitutional monarchy**, a **parliamentary democracy** and a **federal state**
- The phrase **"Peace, Order and Good Government"** is a key phrase in the British North America Act (1867)
- The **Queen** (Crown) is represented by the **Governor General** federally and **Lieutenant Governors** provincially
- **Parliament** consists of the **Senate** (appointed) and the **House of Commons** (elected)
- Canada has **10 provinces** and **3 territories**

### Rights & Freedoms
- Canada's tradition of ordered liberty dates back **800 years** to the **Magna Carta (1215)**
- The **Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms** was entrenched in **1982**
- Key freedoms: freedom of conscience and religion; freedom of thought, belief, opinion and expression (including speech and press); freedom of peaceful assembly; freedom of association
- **Habeas corpus** — the right not to be imprisoned without just cause — is a fundamental right

### National Symbols
- **National flag** — Red and white (colors assigned by King George V in 1921); Maple Leaf design
- **National anthem** — "O Canada"
- **Coat of Arms** — features the motto *A Mari Usque Ad Mare* ("From Sea to Sea") — inspired by Psalm 72
- **Maple leaf** — Canada's symbol since the 1700s; used on cap badges in WWI since the 1850s
- **Beaver** — an early symbol of Canada; national animal
- **RCMP ("the Mounties")** — national police force; founded as NWMP in 1873; one of Canada's best-known symbols
- **Victoria Cross (V.C.)** — highest honour available to Canadians; Canada issues its own V.C. since 1993
- **Remembrance Day poppy** — worn on November 11; inspired by "In Flanders Fields"

### Currency & Portraits
- **$5 bill** — Sir Wilfrid Laurier
- **$10 bill** — Sir John A. Macdonald
- **$20 bill** — The Queen

### Three Founding Peoples
1. **Aboriginal** — First Nations, Métis, Inuit; ancestors migrated from Asia thousands of years ago
2. **French** — Settlers from the 1600s–1700s; descendants form the Québécois and Acadian peoples
3. **British** — English, Welsh, Scottish and Irish settlers from the 1600s to the 20th century; established the basic way of life in English-speaking Canada

### Famous Canadian Quotes
- **Count Frontenac (1690):** *"My only reply will be from the mouths of my cannons!"*
- **John McCrae (1915):** *"In Flanders fields the poppies blow / Between the crosses, row on row…"*
- **Canadian officer at Vimy Ridge (1917):** *"It was Canada from the Atlantic to the Pacific on parade… In those few minutes I witnessed the birth of a nation."*
- **John Buchan / Lord Tweedsmuir (1937):** Immigrants *"should retain their individuality and each make its contribution to the national character."*
